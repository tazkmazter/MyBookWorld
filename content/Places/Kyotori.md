### **KYOTORI**


#### **Geography**

- **The Shatterlands** – Kyotori is an island chain formed by ancient volcanic upheaval. The biggest island, _Haikoru_, holds the high court and mountain temples. The others—_Sunae_, _Tenshi_, _Koryu_—each serve different regional clans and trades.
    
- Frequent mist. Earthquakes that locals treat like passing rain. Forests dense and half-sacred. Rivers that "only flow if you earn it"—seasonal, wild, used in proverbs.
    

#### **Philosophy and Culture**

- The central idea is **Motion-as-Truth**. They believe the _way_ you do something shows more of you than what you say. A slow walk can mean apology, defiance, grief.
    
- **Honor** matters, but less as societal debt (like feudal Japan) and more as _personal symmetry_. Being at odds with your own motion is shameful.
    
- **Memory is sacred**. There are “Listeners” trained to remember stories verbatim across generations. Written history exists but is considered lifeless.
    

#### **Politics and Society**

- Used to be a single empire, now it’s **a fractured council of provinces**. The Empress rules only symbolically. Real power lies with the Five Winds—governors from each region.
    
- Magic is rare and taboo—**a thing of spirits**, not mortals. Those born with power often hide it or are taken to distant monasteries.
    
- **The Outer Children**—a term for those born outside Kyotori to Kyotori blood. Often seen as cursed or destined. That includes Maylene.
    

#### **Style and Custom**

- Clothes: _Uncut robes tied with threads of family color._ Simpler equals higher status—ornament is seen as a kind of inner noise.
    
- Blades: Only carried unsheathed in war or mourning. A drawn blade is a confession.
    
- Tea rituals exist, but it’s **cold tea**, poured from height to honor the mountain spirits that “look down on all.”
    

#### **Myths and Magic**

- Kyotori believes in **Echoes**—spiritual imprints that remain where emotion ran deepest. Old battlegrounds hum. A mother's death cry might linger in a pond.
    
- The **Cradle of Winds** myth says their islands were born when a dragon flung broken stars into the sea, and each became a mountain.
    

#### **Names and Language**

- Let’s develop a naming structure:
    
    - Syllabic rhythm: 2-3 syllables.
        
    - Hard consonant to start (K, T, H) + soft to end (a, u, e).
        
    - Examples: _Harun_, _Mayune_, _Kaeri_, _Sutora_, _Tenshi_, _Hinokai_.

### PHONETICS

Start with a similar base—vowel-heavy, gentle consonants, clean syllables—but tweak the balance.

- Allow _final consonants_ more freely (not just _-n_, like Japanese)
    
- Use more _glottal stops_ or double vowels: e.g. _kai’a_, _touen_, _ur’ta_
    
- Add harsher inflections in military or religious terms: _krashu_, _dorren_, _zukai_
    

### GRAMMAR QUIRKS

Instead of Japanese’s SOV (subject-object-verb) structure, have Kyotori use:

- **OSV** in poetic or formal speech
    
- **VSO** in ritual/magic speech
    
- Allow flexible order in casual usage, but always finish statements with a **verb-particle combo** like:
    
    - _“…goes it shall.”_
        
    - _“…did they, as must be.”_
        

### MORPHOLOGY

Create **suffix-heavy modifiers** but not agglutinative like Japanese:

- Names: _-noa_ (light), _-kai_ (soul), _-ten_ (honor), _-surin_ (of blood)
    
- Adjectives formed by doubling or vowel-shifting: _mai → mairu_ (pure → purified)
    

### CULTURAL HOOKS

Have linguistic registers:

- _High Kyotori_ used in court or old religious texts
    
- _Low Kyotori_ for street talk—more clipped, blunt, and spliced with foreign borrowings
    
- Emphasis on “wind” and “breath” metaphors. Words for “spirit,” “intention,” “presence” should be airy and elusive.

## Scene 

Far across the sea, on the cloud-wreathed cliffs of Kyotori, villagers knelt as the strangers came. Word had spread fast. Lights had danced on the horizon the night before. The wind shifted. The air felt different. By morning, the girls were standing at the edge of the shrine path. No one had seen them arrive.

At first, there was fear. But it didn’t last. The elders spoke of the _kami-tara_—divine echoes who walked like mortals. The girls matched the signs. Amylia’s presence stirred the air with heat. Maylene stood still, sharp and silent. The villagers bowed.

A priest whispered a name long buried in their stories. "Hinokari. The one who bends fire without flame."

Another added, "And the shadow’s edge, who cuts only what fate allows."

The sisters tried to explain. They weren’t divine. They had come to stop a war.

Their denial sealed it. The true _kami-tara_ never claimed divinity. That was the old way.

Drums began. The mountain wind stirred. Temple bells rang for the first time in a hundred years.

They weren’t outsiders now. And they weren’t guests.

They were being worshipped.

And they had no idea what to do about it.
